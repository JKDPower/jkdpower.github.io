# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

JKD practitioners and enthusiasts worldwide — people who already know what the Wednesday Night Group is and want to go deeper, prospective students in Southern California looking to attend class, and curious newcomers who encountered Bruce Lee's name and want to understand the real lineage. A secondary audience: affiliated instructors and schools who treat this site as the authoritative record of the WNG's teaching.

Users arrive with high intent. Nobody stumbles onto a JKD site by accident. They want credibility, history, and a clear answer to "is this the real thing?"

## Product Purpose

Preserve and promote Bruce Lee's original Jeet Kune Do (Jun Fan JKD / "Old School JKD") through the Wednesday Night Group — a 50+ year non-profit practice group in Redlands, California. The site is simultaneously a living community hub and an authoritative archive of lineage, history, and teaching.

Success looks like: a serious JKD student finds this site and immediately trusts they've found the source. A curious newcomer understands why this group is different. A prospective student knows exactly how to apply.

## Brand Personality

Authoritative. Living. Authentic.

Not authority through credentials or corporate polish — authority through a 50-year unbroken chain of practice. Not a museum, but a still-active group that trains every Wednesday night. Not marketing copy, but the real words of real people who knew Bruce Lee.

Voice: direct, unhurried, specific. The tone of someone who has nothing to prove because the lineage speaks for itself.

## References

- **ATA Martial Arts** (atamartialarts.com): professional structure, dark/light contrast, clear program hierarchy, institutional weight. Positive signal: credibility through organization. Negative: franchise-corporate feel to avoid.
- **Martial Arts Websites Design template** (s01-t03.martialartswebsites.design): dark background confidence, bold typography, gold/dark contrast, empathetic copy. Positive signal: the tonal confidence and color commitment.

## Anti-references

- Flashy MMA/UFC-style sites: neon glow effects, aggression-as-aesthetic, fighter silhouettes, energy-drink palette
- Corporate fitness chains: franchise sameness, stock photography, "transformation" marketing language, pricing-first layouts
- Generic karate school sites: clip art belts, Comic Sans legacy, amateur grid layouts

## Design Principles

1. **Lineage is the product.** The 50-year chain from Bruce Lee through Tim Tackett, Bob Bremer, Jim Sewell, and now Jeremy Lynch is the differentiator. Design should make that chain visible and legible — not buried in paragraphs.
2. **Authority through restraint.** Earned trust doesn't need loud selling. The names, the history, the non-profit structure do the work. Design should be confident, not promotional.
3. **Living archive.** The site is both historical record and active community bulletin. It should feel present-tense — a group that still trains — not a memorial.
4. **Show the real thing.** Real people, real photos, real training footage. No generic martial arts silhouettes. No stock photography of strangers in gis.
5. **Accessible depth.** The art has layers; so does the audience. Surface-level visitors need a clear answer to "what is this?" Serious students should be rewarded when they dig.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA minimum. The audience skews older (longtime practitioners) — readable body type, sufficient contrast, no tiny touch targets. No known specific accommodations beyond standard AA compliance.
